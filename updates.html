March 2026 Milestone

I got into Oxford. Here's what that actually means.

I've been officially accepted to Oxford's DPhil program in tumor immunology. I'm still processing what that means — but I know it's the program I've been working toward, and it's real.

What I want to be honest about is that acceptance and arrival are two different things. The cost of a DPhil at Oxford — tuition alone — is £34,700 per year. Add living costs, research expenses, and the logistics of moving internationally, and the gap between "you got in" and "you're actually there doing the science" is significant.

This fundraiser exists because I don't have institutional funding lined up yet, and I didn't want to wait to share what's happening. The science is real. The opportunity is real. The financial gap is also real — and I'd rather be transparent about it than pretend it isn't there.

If you've followed along, donated, or shared this page — thank you. It genuinely matters more than I know how to say.

March 2026 Research

KLF5 results are in — and they're pointing somewhere real.

After months of monitoring our 50-mouse cohort through IVIS imaging, we're starting to see a signal. Preliminary results suggest that KLF5 overexpression accelerates primary tumor progression and increases metastatic burden — while knockouts show the opposite. This positions KLF5 as both a potential prognostic biomarker and a therapeutic target.

There's still a lot of analysis ahead, but seeing functional in vivo data line up with our molecular predictions is genuinely exciting.

One of the harder decisions early on was how to prioritize what to measure. I proposed capturing both terminal metastatic burden and longitudinal tumor kinetics via repeated IVIS imaging — and that decision is now paying off. We can see when the divergence begins, not just where it ends up.

Next steps involve detailed histopathological analysis and additional molecular validation. I'm also starting to write up the methods section for what will hopefully become a manuscript later this year.

January 2026 Community

I started a club, and it already has 30 members.

I founded SOMA — the Systems-Oriented Medical Anatomy Club — at SFSU this semester. Thirty students showed up to the first meeting: pre-med, pre-PA, nursing, and biology students who don't usually share a learning space.

The goal is simple: connect anatomy to its clinical meaning. We run workshops on things like what actually happens during a thoracotomy, or how anatomical variation affects surgical planning. It's the kind of learning I wish I'd had earlier.

What I didn't anticipate was how much students were craving this kind of cross-disciplinary community. Running SOMA alongside my research and teaching load is a lot — but it's also the most energizing thing I do each week. The club is a reminder of why mentorship matters: because someone has to create the spaces that didn't exist when you needed them.

February 2026 Personal

Why I'm doing this publicly.

Research grants fund the science — reagents, animal care, sequencing runs. They don't fund the scientist doing it. Graduate researchers at funded programs typically receive $25,000–35,000/year in stipends. I do equivalent work without that support.

Every day I drive to UCSF's Mission Bay campus costs $150/month in parking alone — on top of student loans, a failing laptop, and application fees that topped $2,000 this cycle. None of that is a personal expense. It's the hidden cost of doing science without institutional backing.

November 2025 Milestone

My first publication is out in Cancer Discovery.

The paper is officially published. I'm listed as a co-author on a study mapping epigenetic and transcriptional programs that define osteosarcoma subtypes — work I contributed to through immunofluorescence experiments and IHC methods sections.

Seeing my name on a Cancer Discovery paper is something I genuinely didn't know was possible two years ago. It matters — not just on a CV, but as proof that the path I'm on is real.
